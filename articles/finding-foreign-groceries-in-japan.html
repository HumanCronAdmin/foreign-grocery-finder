Where to Find Foreign Groceries in Japan: A Local's Guide

Updated April 14, 2026

The first time I moved to Japan, I stood in a Tokyo supermarket aisle staring at a wall of soy sauces. None of them were the one from home. The frustration was instant. Three years later, I still remember the relief of walking into my first import grocery store—finally, a bag of tortilla chips that didn’t taste like cardboard.

The aisles there smelled like home. Not Japan. Not the generic "international" section of a Japanese supermarket. Home. I bought a jar of peanut butter that cost twice as much as it did online, but it didn’t matter. The jar felt like a small victory.

That first jar is still on my shelf. The label is peeling a little. The peanut butter inside is half gone. I don’t buy it anymore—not after I found a cheaper online option—but I keep the empty jar. It’s become a marker. A reminder that the hunt is part of the process.

Finding foreign groceries in Japan isn’t just about locating a store. It’s about understanding the rhythm of the city, learning which neighborhoods have the stores foreigners rely on, and knowing when to compromise. This guide is based on years of trial and error. These are the places I’ve tested, the mistakes I’ve made, and the small tricks that save time and money.

The first mistake people make is assuming all international sections are the same. They’re not. A "foreign foods" section in a Japanese supermarket is often just a shelf with a few Western snacks and maybe some rice-based instant noodles labeled in English. If you’re lucky, you’ll find a jar of Nutella or a bag of pasta. But that’s about it. These sections are designed for tourists and expats passing through—not for people who need specific ingredients regularly.

Another common error is relying solely on online shopping. Online stores like iHerb or Amazon Japan can save time, but shipping costs add up. A jar of peanut butter that costs $5 online might cost $20 by the time it arrives in Japan. If you’re buying in bulk, import fees can double the price. And don’t even get me started on fresh produce. If you want a mango that tastes like the ones from your childhood, online shopping won’t help. You need to know where to look.

The pro tip? Learn the rhythm of your city’s import stores. In Tokyo, certain neighborhoods have clusters of international shops. In Shibuya, there’s a cluster near the station. In Shinjuku, it’s spread out but concentrated near the Golden Gai area. In Osaka, the area around Namba and Umeda has a surprising number of options. Once you know where to look, the hunt becomes easier. You start recognizing the stores like landmarks.

Tokyo: Where to Find Home Groceries

Tokyo is the best city in Japan for finding foreign groceries, but only if you know where to look. The city is big. The options are scattered. And the stores that look promising from the outside often disappoint once you step inside.

The most reliable spot is Ameyoko Market in Ueno. Not the touristy part near the station. Walk past the stalls selling dried squid and cheap socks. Go deeper. There, you’ll find small shops selling imported snacks, spices, and sauces. One shop, called Oriental Bazaar, has a surprising selection of Southeast Asian ingredients. Another, near the fish section, sells fresh produce from Thailand and Vietnam. The prices are reasonable, and the quality is good. But the real gem is a tiny shop tucked between two larger stalls. It sells Latin American ingredients—tortillas, masa harina, canned beans. The owner doesn’t speak much English, but she recognizes regulars. I’ve been going there for three years. She knows I want the same brand of tortilla chips every time.

In Shibuya, the best spot is a small alley behind the station called Center Gai. It’s not a supermarket. It’s not even a store. It’s a collection of tiny shops selling everything from vintage records to imported snacks. One of them, called Shibuya International Market, is a tiny room packed with shelves of imported foods. They sell European chocolates, Middle Eastern spices, and American cereals. The prices are high, but the selection is good. Another shop, near the entrance, sells fresh bread from a French bakery chain. It’s gone by noon, so timing is everything.

For a more traditional supermarket experience, head to Kichijoji. The area around the station has a cluster of international shops. One of them, called National Azabu, is a high-end supermarket with a dedicated foreign foods section. They sell everything from British biscuits to Italian pasta. The prices are high, but the quality is good. Another shop, called Seijo Ishii, is a gourmet supermarket with imported cheeses, meats, and wines. It’s expensive, but if you need a specific ingredient for a recipe, it’s worth the trip.

The pro tip for Tokyo? Learn the train lines that connect the neighborhoods with good import stores. The Yamanote Line connects Shibuya, Shinjuku, and Ueno, making it easy to hop between stores. If you’re in the suburbs, the Tobu Tojo Line connects to Kichijoji. Once you know the lines, the hunt becomes a part of your routine.

Osaka: The Hidden Gems

Osaka isn’t as famous as Tokyo for foreign groceries, but it has hidden gems. The city’s international communities—Korean, Chinese, and Southeast Asian—mean there’s a demand for imported ingredients. And where there’s demand, there are shops.

The best spot is the area around Namba Station. Walk toward Namba Parks and turn left into the smaller streets. There, you’ll find tiny shops selling everything from Korean kimchi to Vietnamese coffee. One shop, called Korea Mart, sells Korean instant noodles, sauces, and frozen dumplings. The prices are reasonable, and the quality is good. Another shop, near the station, sells fresh produce from Thailand and Vietnam. The owner speaks some English and can help if you’re looking for something specific.

For a more traditional supermarket experience, head to Umeda. The area around Hankyu Department Store has a cluster of international shops. One of them, called Hankyu Department Store Food Hall, has a dedicated foreign foods section. They sell everything from British biscuits to Italian pasta. The prices are high, but the quality is good. Another shop, called Osaka Supermarket, is a Korean supermarket with a surprising selection of Japanese and Western ingredients. It’s a bit of a trek from the station, but the selection is worth it.

The pro tip for Osaka? Learn the subway lines. The Midosuji Line connects Namba to Umeda, making it easy to hop between shops. If you’re in the suburbs, the Hankyu Line connects to Hankyu Department Store. Once you know the lines, the hunt becomes easier. You start recognizing the stores like landmarks.

Kyoto and Beyond: Smaller Cities

Outside of Tokyo and Osaka, finding foreign groceries gets harder. The international communities are smaller. The demand is lower. But that doesn’t mean it’s impossible. In Kyoto, the best spot is the area around Kyoto Station. The station has a cluster of international shops selling everything from European chocolates to Asian spices. One shop, called Kyoto International Center, sells imported snacks and sauces. The prices are high, but the quality is good. Another shop, near the station, sells fresh produce from Thailand and Vietnam. The owner speaks some English and can help if you’re looking for something specific.

For a more traditional supermarket experience, head to the Teramachi Shopping Arcade. The area has a cluster of small shops selling everything from Korean kimchi to Japanese instant noodles. One shop, called Kyoto Mart, sells imported snacks and sauces. The prices are reasonable, and the quality is good. Another shop, called Kyoto Supermarket, is a gourmet supermarket with imported cheeses and meats. It’s expensive, but if you need a specific ingredient for a recipe, it’s worth the trip.

The pro tip for Kyoto and smaller cities? Ask at local international centers or community boards. Many cities have small shops catering to the international community, but they’re not always well-known. Sometimes, the best spots are hidden in plain sight. Ask around. Someone will know where to look.

In Fukuoka, the best spot is the area around Tenjin Station. The station has a cluster of international shops selling everything from European chocolates to Asian spices. One shop, called Fukuoka International Center, sells imported snacks and sauces. The prices are high, but the quality is good. Another shop, near the station, sells fresh produce from Thailand and Vietnam. The owner speaks some English and can help if you’re looking for something specific.

Online Shopping: The Pros and Cons

Online shopping can save time, but it comes with trade-offs. The biggest is cost. Shipping fees, import fees, and currency conversion can double the price of groceries. But if you know where to look, online shopping can be a lifesaver.

For Western ingredients, iHerb is a popular option. They sell everything from peanut butter to protein powder. The prices are reasonable, but shipping takes time. If you’re in a hurry, it’s not the best option. Another option is Amazon Japan. They have a surprising selection of imported groceries, but the prices are high. And don’t even get me started on fresh produce. If you want a mango that tastes like the ones from your childhood, online shopping won’t help. You need to know where to look.

The pro tip for online shopping? Check the shipping costs before you buy. Some stores offer free shipping if you spend a certain amount. Others charge flat rates. And if you’re ordering from overseas, check the import fees. Some items, like fresh produce, are restricted. Others, like snacks, are fine but may incur fees. If you’re ordering from the US, consider using a forwarding service like Tenso or Buyee. They’ll forward your order to Japan, often at a lower cost than direct shipping.

Another option is to order from Japanese online stores with international shipping. Stores like Nissen or Rakuten Global Market sell imported groceries and ship internationally. The prices are reasonable, and the selection is good. But again, shipping costs add up. If you’re ordering in bulk, it’s worth it. If you’re ordering a single jar of peanut butter, it’s not.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

One mistake people make is assuming all imported foods are the same. They’re not. A jar of peanut butter from the US tastes different from a jar from Europe. A bag of tortilla chips from Mexico tastes different from a bag from the US. If you’re looking for a specific taste, read the labels carefully. Look for the country of origin. And if possible, ask someone from that country for recommendations.

Another mistake is not checking the expiration dates. Imported foods often have shorter shelf lives than Japanese products. A bag of chips might be fresh when it arrives, but if it sits on the shelf for a month, it might not taste good. Check the dates before you buy. And if you’re ordering online, check the estimated delivery date. If it’s going to take a week to arrive, don’t order something that expires soon.

The pro tip? Learn the Japanese words for "expiration date" and "best before." They’re printed on the packaging. If you’re not sure, ask the store staff. They can help you find the date. And if you’re ordering online, check the product description for the expiration date. Some stores list it; others don’t.

The Small Things That Make a Difference

The hunt for foreign groceries in Japan isn’t just about finding the right store. It’s about learning the small details that make the process easier. It’s about knowing which stores have the best selection, which ones are worth the trip, and which ones to avoid. It’s about building a routine and recognizing the rhythm of the city.

The first time I walked into an import store in Tokyo, I felt like I’d won a small victory. The jar of peanut butter was sitting on the shelf, waiting for me. It wasn’t the same as the one from home. But it was close enough. And that was enough.

Now, when I walk into a store and see the familiar brands, I don’t even think about it. I grab what I need and move on. The hunt has become part of the routine. The frustration has faded. And the small victories still feel like wins.
