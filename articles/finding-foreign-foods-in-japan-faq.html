FAQ: Finding Home Foods in Japan as a Foreigner

Updated April 13, 2026

What’s the easiest way to find international grocery stores in Japan

Start with the largest chains. National Azabu in Tokyo has branches in Hiroo and Roppongi. Their flagship store in Hiroo is a maze of imported goods—from German chocolate to Mexican salsa. Osaka’s Kansai Super chain covers the western region well, with dedicated sections for Asian, European, and American products. Smaller cities often have at least one Seiyu or AEON with an “International Foods” aisle, though selection varies wildly depending on location.

The app Tabelog isn’t just for restaurant reviews. Search for keywords like “インターナショナルスーパー” or “海外食材” and filter by your area. Users often leave detailed photos of store layouts, which helps when you’re hunting for a specific item. A common mistake is assuming all Aeon stores carry the same selection—some smaller branches have shelves labeled “International Foods” that are nearly empty. Always check the Tabelog listing photos before heading out.

Another reliable option is Amazon Japan. Their “International Foods” category isn’t limited to Japanese sellers. Search for your target country’s snacks or ingredients, then filter by “Ships from overseas.” Fees vary: expect ¥500–¥2,000 for standard shipping, with delivery taking 3–15 days depending on the seller’s location. Some sellers offer free shipping above ¥3,000, which can offset the cost of larger orders. The downside? Customs fees on some shipments can push the total higher than expected. Always check the “total amount after fees” before confirming.

Pro tip: Join local Facebook groups like “Foreign Groceries in [Your City]” or “Expats in Osaka/Kyoto.” Members often share photos of new store finds or alert each other when their local Seiyu restocks international items. These groups move faster than forums and include both long-term expats and newcomers who’ve just discovered a hidden gem. One member in Fukuoka recently posted about a tiny shop in Tenjin called Kokusai Foods that carries Scandinavian products—something even Google Maps missed.

How much can I expect to spend on imported groceries

The price gap between domestic and imported foods in Japan is real. A 100g bag of authentic French cheese at National Azabu runs ¥1,200–¥1,800. The same cheese at a local supermarket costs ¥600–¥800. Imported snacks follow the same pattern: a bag of Swedish cinnamon buns is ¥400 at Aeon versus ¥300 at a regular store. The markup isn’t just profit—customs duties, storage costs, and limited shelf space all factor in.

Seasonal items can swing wildly. During Lunar New Year, Chinese ingredients at Yaohan Market in Yokohama see a 30–50% price increase. The same goes for European chocolates around Christmas. If you’re planning a bulk order, time your purchase for right after holidays when stores discount leftover stock. Another mistake people make is assuming all imported products are expensive. Canned goods, dried pasta, and spices often cost the same as domestic versions. It’s the fresh or gourmet items that carry the premium.

For budgeting, track your spending for a month. Most expats find they spend ¥15,000–¥25,000 monthly on imported groceries if they shop at mid-range stores like Kaldi or Seijo Ishii. Those who rely on Amazon or direct overseas orders can drop below ¥10,000 but risk customs fees or longer wait times. A local in Kobe once told me he saves by buying frozen dumplings at Daiei—the Chinese brand versions are ¥300 per pack versus ¥500 for fresh ones at international stores.

Which stores specialize in specific cuisines

For European foods, Seijo Ishii is king. Their flagship store in Den-en-chōfu, Tokyo, spans two floors of French, Italian, and German products. The cheese section alone takes up an entire aisle. Kaldi is the budget-friendly alternative, with smaller but well-curated European selections in most locations. Their Kanagawa branches tend to have better stock than rural areas.

Asian groceries have more fragmented options. Yaohan Market in Yokohama covers Chinese, Korean, and Southeast Asian items under one roof. The basement level of their Chiba branch is a favorite for Thai ingredients. For Korean foods, Namba City in Osaka has a dedicated floor called “Korean Town,” though it’s more tourist-focused. Smaller cities often rely on local Asian markets—ask in expat groups for hidden spots like Tokyo’s Shin-Okubo for Korean goods or Osaka’s Tsuruhashi for Japanese-Korean fusion ingredients.

Middle Eastern and African products are harder to find. Tokyo’s Nakameguro has a small but reliable Middle Eastern grocery called Aladdin Foods. Their website lists locations in Yokohama and Kawasaki as well. For African ingredients, African Market Tokyo in Ikebukuro is the only dedicated spot I’ve found—stock is limited but includes staples like palm oil and jollof rice mixes. A common error is assuming all international foods are in one place. Some stores specialize deeply in one cuisine but have almost nothing else. Always check their website or call ahead.

Pro tip: Learn the Japanese terms for your target cuisine’s aisle. “欧州食品” (European foods), “アジア食品” (Asian foods), and “中東食品” (Middle Eastern foods) are the most common labels. Some stores use “輸入食品” (imported foods) as a catch-all, which often means the selection is random. If you’re looking for something specific, call the store first. A staff member in Fukuoka once told me their “International Foods” section only carries snacks, not baking ingredients—something the online listing didn’t clarify.

How do customs work when ordering from overseas

Japan’s customs process is straightforward but can feel opaque. When an international order arrives, you’ll get a 通関手続き書類 (customs procedure form) in your mailbox. This isn’t a bill—it’s a notification that duty may apply. The form lists the item’s value, origin country, and estimated tax. You have 15 days to either pay the duty at the post office or return the package. Ignoring it results in the package being sent back to the sender.

The duty threshold is ¥10,000 for personal imports. Below that, no tax is applied. Above it, rates vary: alcohol and tobacco carry higher duties, while most food items fall under 10–20%. For example, a $30 box of American cookies from Amazon US might incur a ¥800–¥1,200 duty. Shipping fees are taxed separately—a $5 shipping charge adds another ¥100–¥200. The total often surprises newcomers who assume “free shipping” means no extra costs.

A mistake people make is not checking the “dutiable” checkbox on some sellers’ checkout pages. Some US-based shops automatically declare your order as a gift to avoid duties, but this is risky—Japan Customs can still assess taxes if they suspect the value is misrepresented. Another pitfall is perishable items. Frozen foods or dairy products often get held up at customs for inspection, resulting in thawed or spoiled goods by the time they reach you. Always check the seller’s customs policy before ordering.

Pro tip: Use a forwarding service like Tenso or Buyee if you’re ordering from sites that don’t ship to Japan. These services give you a Japanese address to use during checkout, then forward the package internationally. Fees vary: Tenso charges ¥500–¥1,500 depending on package size, while Buyee’s fees depend on the auction site’s cut. The advantage is consolidating multiple orders into one shipment, which can lower shipping costs. Just be aware that some sellers on eBay or Mercari won’t ship to forwarding services—always message the seller first.

Are there stores that sell halal or kosher foods

Halal options are more common than kosher, thanks to Japan’s Muslim communities and tourism. Tokyo’s Ikebukuro has a small halal grocery called Halal Mart, stocking Middle Eastern spices, rice, and frozen meats. Their website lists branches in Osaka and Kyoto as well. For halal-certified snacks, National Azabu and Kaldi often carry brands like “Halal Gummy” or “Soy Joy.” Always check the packaging—some products labeled “halal-style” aren’t officially certified.

Kosher foods are rarer. Jerusalem Market in Tokyo’s Shinjuku district is the closest option, though their selection is limited to dried goods and canned items. The owner, an Israeli expat, sources products directly from Israel. For fresh kosher meats, your best bet is to contact local synagogues or Jewish community centers—they often arrange group orders from overseas butcheries. A common error is assuming “kosher” translates directly. Some products labeled “kosher” in Japan follow Sephardic traditions, which differ from Ashkenazi standards common in the US or Europe.

Pro tip: Download the app Halal Navi for a map of halal-certified restaurants and stores across Japan. It’s not just for food—some mosques and prayer rooms are marked too. The app’s user reviews often mention whether a store’s halal certification is up to date, which is crucial since some places let their certifications lapse. For kosher travelers, the Chabad House in Tokyo or Osaka can point you to the nearest kosher options or arrange deliveries.

What should I do if I can’t find a specific ingredient

Start with substitutions. Japanese grocery stores carry some surprising alternatives. For example, Calrose rice is often labeled as “uruchi-mai” and works as a substitute for short-grain rice in most recipes. If you’re missing a specific spice, Spice Garden in Tokyo’s Daikanyama neighborhood sells single-ingredient spices in small jars. Their online store ships within Japan, which is useful if you’re in a rural area.

Another approach is to ask in expat groups. Someone might have a stash or know where to order online. A member in Sapporo recently asked about Swedish lingonberry jam—another user responded with a link to a Tokyo-based seller on Mercari who had a surplus from a canceled order. For baking ingredients, Surfas in Tokyo’s Jiyugaoka is a hidden gem. Their online store ships nationwide and carries specialty flours, chocolate, and extracts that regular supermarkets lack.

A mistake people make is giving up too soon. Some ingredients appear only during specific seasons or at certain stores. Mexican chocolate shows up around Día de los Muertos in October but disappears by November. If you need it year-round, your best bet is to order from an overseas seller like MexGrocer or La Tienda. Shipping takes 2–4 weeks, but the quality is consistent. For fresh produce, your local farmers’ market might surprise you—some stalls sell imported apples or grapes imported from New Zealand or Chile during off-seasons.

Pro tip: Learn the Japanese name for your ingredient and its common substitutes. For example, “cinnamon” is “シナモン” (shinamon), but if you’re missing it, “肉桂” (nikkei, cassia bark) is a close alternative available in most Japanese spice aisles. Carry a small cheat sheet with you when shopping—some store staff won’t understand English terms, even for common items. Another insider trick is to check the “海外直送” (overseas direct) section on Amazon Japan. Some third-party sellers offer same-day shipping within Japan by consolidating orders from overseas warehouses. It’s not always cheaper, but it’s faster than waiting for international delivery.

How do I check if a store is still open or has stock

Call ahead. Most international grocery stores in Japan have websites, but their stock lists aren’t always updated. A quick phone call saves a wasted trip. National Azabu’s Hiroo branch has a bilingual staff line—dial their main number and press 2 for English support. Smaller shops like Halal Mart in Ikebukuro don’t always list their hours online, so calling is essential. Some stores close early on Sundays or holidays, especially in rural areas.

Another method is to check Google Maps reviews. Look for recent photos uploaded within the last month—these often show current stock or layout changes. A common error is relying on old forum posts. A 2023 thread about Kansai Super’s Osaka branch claiming it carries a wide selection of Korean goods might no longer be accurate. Stores rotate their international sections frequently based on demand and supplier availability.

Pro tip: Follow international grocery stores on Instagram or Facebook. Many post daily stock updates or limited-time offers. Seijo Ishii, for example, posts photos of new European cheeses arriving every Thursday. Their Tokyo store also offers curbside pickup for online orders, which is useful if you’re short on time. Another insider move is to sign up for their email newsletters—some stores send alerts when they restock popular items like French butter or Italian pasta.
